Can we shrink the models without losing accuracy?

Your lead said yes. The research paper mostly agrees. Here's what that actually means — in plain English.

The short answer

Q: Can we make the AI models smaller without making them dumber?

A: Mostly yes. We can usually shrink them by 2× to 4× and lose less than 1% accuracy. "Zero loss" is rare. "Tiny loss you probably can't see" is normal.

So your lead is right in spirit. The honest version is: "shrink a lot, lose a little." Not "shrink a lot, lose nothing."

Why models can be shrunk at all

Think of an AI model like a giant spreadsheet full of numbers. Most of those numbers are stored with way more precision than they actually need — like writing your weight as 72.4738291847 kg when 72.5 kg is fine.

Shrinking a model means writing those numbers in a less wasteful way. The model still works. It just takes up less space and runs faster.

The 4 main ways to shrink a model

1. Quantization — use simpler numbers

Like rounding 72.4738 kg to 72.5 kg. Still close enough.

The model's numbers are stored as 32-bit decimals. We replace them with 16-bit decimals (FP16) or even 8-bit whole numbers (INT8). The model gets 2× to 4× smaller and runs faster.

2–4× smaller Under 1% accuracy lost Easy to try

2. Pruning — delete the parts that don't matter

Like trimming dead branches off a tree. The tree still grows.

Most of the connections inside a neural network barely contribute. We can find them and delete them. One paper (DropNet) cut 90% of a model away with no accuracy drop.

Up to 10× smaller Needs careful tuning

3. Knowledge Distillation — teacher and student

A senior engineer teaching a junior. The junior is faster, almost as good.

Train a small "student" model to copy what the big "teacher" model does. The student is much smaller but performs nearly the same on the task we care about.

2–10× smaller Needs retraining

4. Use a smaller model from the start

Buy a Honda Civic instead of trying to shrink a truck.

Models like MobileNet, EfficientNet, and ShuffleNet are built from the ground up to be small and fast. If we ever retrain, we should retrain on these.

Designed for phones Means retraining

What the research paper actually said

The paper you sent (Wang et al., March 2025) is a survey — meaning it doesn't run new experiments, it summarizes what 280+ other papers found. The repeated phrasing throughout is:

  • "without significantly compromising accuracy"
  • "with minimal accuracy loss"
  • "while maintaining competitive accuracy"

Notice it says minimal, not zero. That's the honest framing. The one place it gets close to "zero" is DropNet — 90% smaller, "without compromising accuracy." That's one paper, one type of model, one dataset.

What this means for RackTrack

Our models total 396 MB. That's too big to ship in an app. If we use quantization (the easiest method), we'd realistically get to around 100 MB total — still big, but shippable as a post-install download.

But here's the catch: every paper that claims "minimal loss" tested on their models, on their data. Nobody has tested on our specific rack-detection models with our specific rack photos. Until we measure, we don't know.

Bottom line

Your lead is right that this is possible. The research strongly supports it. But we still need to actually try it on one of our models and measure the result — because "minimal loss" only becomes a real number after we run the experiment.

That's exactly the one-week test in the original brief. Start with the smallest model (EfficientNet, 15.6 MB), shrink it, measure accuracy, see what happens.
